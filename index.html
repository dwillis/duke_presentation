Mr. Wells

My title comes from a 1943 speech by H.G. Wells to the British Association Conference that might be best remembered for his advocacy of burning low-quality books, but he began it by addressing an existenial crisis in journalism: "what we have known hitherto as the newspaper is dead as mutton and it will never come back. we are sailing toward a new disorder or a new order in the world. if it is disorder, it will not tolerate a newspaper, and if it's order, it will have no need for it." Journalists who can do their entire jobs on a tablet or smart phone are underachieving. Journalism students who are taught that they won't need math are being set up for failure. The way most reporters treat information could qualify as malpractice. The promises of technology are less a cause of journalism's problems than an opportunity for better reporting and story-telling. Today there is no single formula for how to do journalism, and no single form of it. Thus, journalists need to be more than just users of technology; they need to be involved in the creation of systems built for their unique duties. Although not every journalism job can be improved via automated processes, computers can greatly improve our work by providing broader context, ever-vigilant sentinels for news or the ability to test theories - our own and our sources - before publication. This talk is about some of the ways that computing informs reporting at The New York Times and other news organizations, and about the mostly unrealized opportunities for software and hardware engineers to make journalism better - and to do some of it themselves.
